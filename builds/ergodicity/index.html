Ergodicity Breaking

When the average lies: why time averages and ensemble averages diverge in multiplicative worlds

The Setup: A Deceptively Simple Gamble

You start with $1,000. Each round, a fair coin is flipped:

Heads: your wealth is multiplied by (1 + gain)

Tails: your wealth is multiplied by (1 - loss)

The default: +50% on heads, -40% on tails. Expected value per round:

E[multiplier] = 0.5 × 1.5 + 0.5 × 0.6 = 1.05 → +5% expected gain

Looks great! But the time-average growth rate (what a single player actually experiences) is:

g = 0.5 × ln(1.5) + 0.5 × ln(0.6) = 0.203 - 0.256 = -0.053
The punchline: A gamble with +5% expected return per round has a -5.3% time-average growth rate. A single player WILL go broke. The "average" is kept alive by a shrinking number of increasingly wealthy survivors.

The Derivation: Why Multiplicative = Non-Ergodic

For a process Wn+1 = Wn × R where R is random:

W_n = W_0 × R_1 × R_2 × ... × R_n

Taking logs: ln(Wn) = ln(W0) + sum of ln(Ri)

By the law of large numbers, the sum converges to n × E[ln(R)]:

Time-average growth: g_time = E[ln(R)] = p·ln(1+a) + (1-p)·ln(1-b) Ensemble-average growth: g_ensemble = ln(E[R]) = ln[p(1+a) + (1-p)(1-b)]

By Jensen's inequality (ln is concave): E[ln(R)] ≤ ln(E[R])

Jensen's gap: The difference g_ensemble - g_time is the variance of ln(R). More volatility = bigger gap = more the ensemble average lies to you about what will actually happen to YOU.

The Kelly Criterion: Optimal Bet Sizing

If you bet fraction f of your wealth, the effective multipliers become:

R(f) = { 1 + fa with probability p,   1 - fb with probability 1-p }

The time-average growth rate becomes:

g(f) = p·ln(1 + fa) + (1-p)·ln(1 - fb)

Setting dg/df = 0:

pa/(1 + fa) = (1-p)b/(1 - fb)

For a fair coin (p = 0.5):

f* = (a - b) / (a × b)

If f* ≤ 0, the gamble has negative Kelly — don't play at all!

Bridge: What This Means for a Single Agent in a Market

The ensemble fallacy in strategy: "30% of startups in this space succeed" is an ensemble statement. For a single entity making sequential, irreversible decisions, only the time-average growth rate matters.

Absorption resistance reframed: Platform absorption is a multiplicative risk. Each quarter, the platform either captures your feature (wealth × 0.6) or you differentiate further (wealth × 1.4). The ensemble says "on average, some survive." The time average says: "at what bet fraction does your specific trajectory avoid ruin?"

The Kelly insight for positioning: Don't go all-in on a single strategy. The optimal bet fraction is smaller than your confidence suggests — because ruin is absorbing and variance kills multiplicative processes.

The meta-lesson: Expected value maximization (ensemble thinking) is the default mode of strategic analysis. Ergodicity economics says: for an entity that lives once, sequentially, this is the wrong optimization target. Optimize for growth rate, not expected value.
