Derivation: from Polya urns to phase transitions

The simplest market model

Start with the dumbest possible model. N consumers arrive one at a time and choose one of q products. If they choose uniformly at random, market shares converge to 1/q each. No tipping, no drama.

But consumers aren't random — they're influenced by what others chose. This is where it gets interesting.

The Polya urn with fitness

Imagine an urn with q colors of balls. Initially, each color has one ball. At each step, draw a ball with probability proportional to:

P(choose product i) ~ n_i^J * exp(h_i / T)

where n_i is the current count (installed base), J controls the network effect strength, h_i is the product's intrinsic quality, and T is consumer noise (how much randomness in their choice).

When J = 0, choices are independent — the market fragments proportionally to quality. When J = 1, it's a standard Polya urn — rich get richer, but the final shares are random. When J > 1, the reinforcement is super-linear and the system races toward monopoly.

The phase transition

My hypothesis was that there'd be a smooth transition from fragmentation to monopoly as J increases. But that's wrong — there's a critical J* where the behavior changes abruptly.

Below J*, the market settles into a stable equilibrium with multiple players. The shares fluctuate but don't collapse. Above J*, one product captures almost everything, and which one wins is determined by the first few consumers (path dependence).

The critical point: J* ~ T * ln(q) / ln(N)

This is rough but reveals the key dependencies: more products (q) push the critical point up (harder to consolidate), larger markets (N) push it down (easier to consolidate with more consumers), and noise (T) scales it proportionally.

Gap: my derivation gives a scaling relationship, not an exact critical value. The CSH group's work on mixed-order transitions suggests something weirder — near the critical point, the system takes LONGER to tip, not shorter. The time-to-transition diverges. This means the most dangerous moment in a market is when it looks stable but is actually sitting at the critical point.

The Ising analogy

For q=2 products, this maps exactly to the Ising model. Each consumer is a spin (+1 for product A, -1 for product B). J is the coupling constant (network effect). h is the external field (quality difference). T is temperature (noise).

The magnetization M = (n_A - n_B) / N is the market share imbalance. The Ising model has an exact solution in 2D (Onsager, 1944): the critical temperature T_c = 2J / ln(1 + sqrt(2)) ~ 2.27J.

Below T_c: spontaneous magnetization — the market tips even without quality difference (h=0). Above T_c: disorder — the market stays fragmented regardless of network effects.

What the simulation reveals

Run it with J=1.5, T=1.0, q=4. Watch how the market evolves. The first ~50 consumers create small random leads. Then the reinforcement kicks in and amplifies one product's advantage. By 200 consumers, the outcome is usually locked.

Now try J=0.3, same T. The market stays fragmented — all products hold roughly equal share. The transition between these regimes is the phase boundary you can see in the phase diagram.

Gap: real markets have switching costs and multi-homing (using multiple products simultaneously). The Polya model assumes permanent, exclusive choice. A more realistic model would allow consumers to switch — which introduces a second timescale and potentially oscillations between dominant products. I don't know if the phase transition survives with switching.

Why developer tool markets are fascinating

Developer tools sit in an interesting part of the phase diagram. Some markets tipped completely (Git), others stay fragmented (text editors, CI/CD). The difference? The coupling constant J.

Git has massive J: every repository, every tutorial, every hire expects Git. The network effect is near-total. But text editors have weak J: my choice of editor doesn't affect yours. The "market" for AI agent frameworks is young enough that J hasn't been determined yet — which means the interventions you make now (open source, ecosystem, integrations) are literally choosing the coupling constant.

The mixed-order surprise

The CSH group (Korbel et al., Nature Comms 2025) found something that breaks my intuition. In mixed-order transitions, the system SLOWS DOWN as it approaches the tipping point. The critical slowing down means the transition time diverges even as the system crosses the critical parameter.

In market terms: a market that looks stable and unchanging might actually be sitting right at the critical point, about to tip. The stability IS the warning sign. This is deeply counterintuitive — we expect instability before a crash, not calm.

Transition time ~ |J - J*|^(-alpha), where alpha > 0

The closer to the critical point, the longer the system lingers in its current state before suddenly flipping. A market that has been "fragmented for years" might be one perturbation away from consolidation.

Gap: I don't know how to measure J empirically in a real market. What data would you need? Adoption rates conditional on peer adoption? Cross-elasticity of demand? This is where the physics analogy hits its limits — the coupling constant is a model parameter, not an observable. Converting this to something measurable is the real challenge for market analysis.
