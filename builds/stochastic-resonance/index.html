Derivation from First Principles

1. The setup: a particle in a double-well

Consider a particle with overdamped dynamics in a quartic potential:

U(x) = -x2/2 + x4/4

This has two stable minima at x = +/-1 and an unstable maximum at x = 0. The barrier height is Delta U = 1/4.

We drive this system with a weak periodic signal and noise:

dx/dt = x - x3 + A0cos(omega t) + sqrt(2D) xi(t)

where xi(t) is Gaussian white noise and D is the noise intensity. The signal amplitude A0 is weak: too small to push the particle over the barrier alone.

2. Kramers escape rate: how noise moves the particle

Without the signal, the particle sits in one well. Noise occasionally kicks it over the barrier. Kramers (1940) showed the mean escape rate is:

rK = (1/2pi) sqrt(|U''(xmin)| x |U''(xmax)|) x exp(-Delta U / D)

For our potential: U''(+/-1) = 2, |U''(0)| = 1, Delta U = 1/4. So:

rK = sqrt(2) / (2pi) x exp(-1/(4D))
Key insight: The escape rate is exponentially sensitive to noise. A small increase in D can dramatically change switching frequency. This exponential lever is what makes resonance possible.

3. The resonance condition: when noise and signal synchronize

The signal periodically tilts the potential, making one well shallower and the other deeper. If noise-driven switching happens at roughly the same rate as the signal frequency, the particle "surfs" the signal — switching wells in time with each half-cycle.

The matching condition is:

2 rK ~ omega / (2pi)

Solving for the optimal noise:

Dopt ~ 1 / (4 ln(2 sqrt(2) / omega))

4. Signal-to-noise ratio: the peak

The output SNR (power at frequency omega divided by background noise power) is:

SNR proportional to (A02 / D2) x rK

Two competing effects:

  • D -> 0: rK -> 0 exponentially. No switching, no output at all. Too quiet.
  • D -> infinity: 1/D2 -> 0. Random switching drowns the signal. Too loud.
  • At D = Dopt: the sweet spot. Noise-driven transitions are phase-locked to the signal. Just right.
This is stochastic resonance: There exists an optimal noise intensity — not zero! — that maximizes the system's ability to detect a weak signal. The noise is not a nuisance to be eliminated. It's a resource to be tuned.

5. Why it's wild: from ice ages to stink bugs

Benzi, Parisi, Sutera, and Vulpiani (1981) invented this framework to explain a mystery of paleoclimatology: the Earth's ice ages have a ~100,000-year periodicity, yet the Milankovitch cycles (orbital variations) that drive them are far too weak to force such large climate transitions alone. SR says: the climate system is bistable (glacial/interglacial), the orbital forcing is sub-threshold, and internal climate noise provides the stochastic boost.

Forty-five years later, a 2026 paper found double stochastic resonance in stink bug mating communication — environmental noise helping both the sender's signal and the receiver's detection simultaneously.

The mechanism is the same everywhere: nonlinearity + threshold + noise = amplification. A neuron firing, an ice age beginning, a bug finding a mate.
